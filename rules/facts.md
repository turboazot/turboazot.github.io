# Facts and Proof

## 1. Replace evaluation with evidence

Do not write that something is:

- useful
- effective
- quality
- convenient
- strong
- unique
- professional
- modern
- innovative

These words explain nothing.

Replace them with:

- numbers
- concrete details
- observable effects
- examples
- comparisons

Bad:
> This service offers a high-quality user experience.

Better:
> A new user can create an invoice in under three minutes without reading documentation.

Bad:
> The team built an innovative onboarding system.

Better:
> The new onboarding flow cut the drop-off rate after signup from 42% to 25%.

## 2. Use numbers when they change understanding

A number is useful when it helps the reader judge scale, speed, money, or impact.

Good:

- conversion grew from 2.1% to 3.4%
- the article got 12,000 reads in a week
- support answered in 8 minutes on average
- the team cut the form from 14 fields to 6

If you do not have the number, do not fake certainty.
Use `[XX]` or rewrite the claim honestly.

## 3. Add examples early

A reader understands faster when the article shows a case early.

Weak pattern:
- theory for six paragraphs
- example at the end

Stronger pattern:
- point
- example
- explanation

Bad:
> Good onboarding creates trust.

Better:
> Good onboarding creates trust. For example, Miro starts with one small action: it asks you to place one sticky note on the board. The user feels progress before reading any documentation.

## 4. Use details that change the picture

A strong detail is not decoration. It changes how the reader sees the scene.

Weak:
> The office was modern.

Strong:
> Every meeting room had a timer on the wall that turned red at the 50-minute mark.

Weak:
> The landing page looked messy.

Strong:
> The first screen had three buttons, two forms, a chatbot pop-up, and no clear headline.

## 5. Separate fact, interpretation, and opinion

Do not disguise opinion as fact.

- Fact: what happened
- Interpretation: what it may mean
- Opinion: what you think about it

Bad:
> Users hated the redesign.

Better:
> After the redesign, daily usage fell by 18%. This likely means the new navigation confused existing users.

## 6. Be careful with comparisons

A comparison is useful when it helps the reader orient.

Good:
> This editor is closer to Notion than to Google Docs: it is better for structuring notes than for formal document exchange.

Bad:
> This is like Uber, but for everything.

## 7. Show the consequence

Facts become stronger when the reader sees their effect.

Bad:
> The company reduced the form length.

Better:
> The company reduced the form from 14 fields to 6, and completed applications grew by 27%.
